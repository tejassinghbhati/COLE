How this works

A plain-language tour of the data, the machine-learning model, and how every number and chart on screen is produced. For the full scientific deep-dive — with the SHAP maths, diagrams and formulas — see docs/HOW_IT_WORKS.md.

1 · What this is

The Cost of Living Explainer answers a question that a single price tag cannot: why is a city expensive, and who can actually afford to live there? It pairs a predictive model with an explainability layer, so every estimate comes with a breakdown of the forces behind it — not just a number.

2 · The data

Everything is built on real data from Numbeo's Cost of Living Index by City — the most widely cited cross-city reference, aggregated from crowd-sourced prices. The snapshot covers 545 cities across 116 countries.

Numbeo publishes its indices relative to New York City = 100. This app re-bases everything to New Delhi = 100: each index is divided by New Delhi's value and multiplied by 100. So New Delhi reads 100 on every axis, and a city scoring 300 is roughly three times as expensive as Delhi. The columns used are rent, groceries, restaurants, the overall total (cost of living including rent), and local purchasing power.

3 · The model

An XGBoost gradient-boosted-tree regressor predicts a city's total cost of living from four inputs — rent, groceries, restaurants and local purchasing power — plus its world region. Gradient-boosted trees are the state of the art for this kind of tabular data and capture interactions between factors natively.

The model is trained on 80% of the cities and tested on the 20% it never saw. On that hold-out set it reaches R² ≈ 0.99 (the share of variation it explains) with an average error of about 6.5 index points.

4 · Explaining each prediction (SHAP)

A prediction alone teaches nothing. We use SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations), rooted in cooperative game theory, to fairly divide each prediction among its inputs. Every estimate is split into a base value (the typical city) plus a contribution from each factor.

Those contributions are the driver bars: a rust bar pushes a city above the typical city, a teal bar pulls it below. Across all cities, rent & housing is the single largest driver of total cost — the central real-world finding this tool surfaces.

5 · Similar cities

"Most similar cities" is a separate, unsupervised step: a k-nearest-neighbours search over the standardized cost features. It matches the shape of a city's costs — which categories run high or low — rather than just the headline total, so two equally pricey cities can still be very different.

6 · The per-city overview

Each city gets a short written summary grounded in its real numbers. When an Anthropic API key is configured, these are written by Claude (Haiku); otherwise a deterministic, data-driven template produces the same facts so the app works fully offline. Either way they are generated once and cached, then served instantly.

7 · The world map

All 545 cities were geocoded by joining the dataset to two open references (SimpleMaps and GeoNames) — a 100% match. The interactive map plots only these cities, each dot coloured by total cost (teal → rust). Zoom, pan, hover for a tooltip, and click to load a city. The map, the dropdown and the similar-cities list all stay in sync.

8 · Reading each tab

  • Explore a city — pick a city (map or dropdown) to see its overview, metrics, SHAP driver bars and nearest neighbours.
  • Compare — set two cities and see, category by category, exactly where the cost gap comes from.
  • Global insights — the factors that drive cost worldwide, a regional summary, and the model's accuracy.
  • Custom profile — move sliders to invent a city and watch the model and its SHAP explanation react.

9 · Honest limitations

  • Numbeo is crowd-sourced; coverage and accuracy vary by city and drift over time. The snapshot is a point-in-time reading.
  • SHAP explains the model, which captures associations — these are not controlled causal effects.
  • The high R² is partly structural: the target (total cost) is itself a basket of the input categories, so the interesting output is the decomposition, not the raw accuracy.
  • A single city index hides neighbourhood-level inequality; purchasing power mitigates but does not remove this.
