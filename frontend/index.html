A technical monograph

The Mechanics of an Explainable Cost-of-Living Model

How real price data becomes a prediction, an explanation, and a map.

Abstract

This document describes, from first principles, how the Cost of Living Explainer works. We take a real cross-city price dataset, re-express it on a common scale anchored at New Delhi, and fit a gradient-boosted regression model to predict the total cost of living. We then decompose every prediction into the contribution of each underlying cost using Shapley values, complement the model with an unsupervised similarity search, and narrate each city with a language model. The emphasis throughout is on explanation rather than mere prediction: the goal is not only to estimate how expensive a city is, but to make that estimate legible.

Chapter 1

Motivation

The cost of living is among the most tangible of socioeconomic quantities, yet it is routinely communicated as a single opaque figure. To say that a city's index is three hundred conveys magnitude but not structure: it does not tell us whether the expense is driven by rent, by food, or by the price of a meal out, nor whether local wages keep pace. The ambition of this project is to recover that structure.

We therefore treat the problem not as forecasting — estimating an unknown future price — but as decomposition: given a city's prevailing prices, attribute its overall cost of living to its constituent parts. This reframing is what justifies the machinery that follows. A predictive model supplies the estimate; an attribution method supplies the explanation; together they turn a number into an argument.

Chapter 2

The data and its provenance

The dataset is drawn from Numbeo's Cost of Living Index by City, the most widely cited cross-city reference, which aggregates crowd-sourced prices for roughly ninety goods and services per city into category indices. The working snapshot comprises 545 cities across 116 countries.

Six published columns are used: a cost-of-living index excluding rent, a rent index, their combination (the cost of living including rent), a groceries index, a restaurant-price index, and a local-purchasing-power index that reflects what a typical net salary can buy. Numbeo expresses all of these relative to a fixed reference city.

Index. A relative price level. If city A has a groceries index of 200 and the reference city is 100, groceries in A are, on average, twice as expensive. Indices are unitless and comparable across cities by construction.
Chapter 3

Re-basing to a common origin

Numbeo anchors its scale at New York City. We re-anchor it at New Delhi, so that every quantity is read as a multiple of Delhi's. For a city $c$ and column $k$, the re-based index is

$$ I^{\text{Delhi}}_{c,k} \;=\; \frac{I^{\text{NYC}}_{c,k}}{I^{\text{NYC}}_{\text{Delhi},k}}\,\times\,100. $$

By construction New Delhi equals one hundred on every axis, and a city scoring three hundred is approximately three times as expensive. Re-basing is a linear change of units; it alters no relationship in the data, but it makes the scale interpretable against a familiar South-Asian baseline rather than a North-American one.

Chapter 4

Problem formulation

We pose a supervised regression. Each city is a feature vector $x \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ comprising its rent, groceries, restaurant and purchasing-power indices together with a one-hot encoding of its world region. The target $y$ is the total cost of living including rent. We seek a function $f$ such that $f(x)\approx y$, learned from the observed cities.

Why region is encoded, not averaged. Region is categorical; a model cannot consume the string "Western Europe". One-hot encoding turns each region into an independent indicator the model may use or ignore, preserving its distinct effect.
Chapter 5

The model: gradient-boosted trees

The estimator is a gradient-boosted ensemble of regression trees, implemented with XGBoost. The prediction is an additive sum of $K$ trees,

$$ \hat{y}_i \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} f_k(x_i), \qquad f_k \in \mathcal{F}, $$

where $\mathcal{F}$ is the space of regression trees. The trees are fit by minimising a regularised objective that balances fit against complexity,

$$ \mathcal{L} \;=\; \sum_i \ell\!\left(y_i, \hat{y}_i\right) \;+\; \sum_k \Omega(f_k), \qquad \Omega(f) = \gamma T + \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda \lVert w \rVert^{2}, $$

in which $\ell$ is the squared error, $T$ the number of leaves in a tree, $w$ its leaf weights, and $\gamma,\lambda$ penalise complexity. Trees are added one at a time; each new tree is fit to the second-order approximation of the loss, and a learning rate $\eta$ shrinks its contribution so the ensemble generalises:

$$ \hat{y}^{(t)}_i \;=\; \hat{y}^{(t-1)}_i + \eta\, f_t(x_i). $$

Gradient-boosted trees are chosen deliberately. They are the prevailing method for structured, tabular data of this size; they model interactions between features without manual specification; they are invariant to the scale of inputs; and, crucially, they admit an exact attribution method (Chapter 7). The configuration used favours many shallow trees — five hundred trees of depth four, a learning rate of $0.05$, and row and column subsampling — a regime that captures interaction while resisting overfitting.

Chapter 6

Training and evaluation

The cities are partitioned: four-fifths are used to fit the model and one-fifth are held out, untouched, to estimate generalisation. Two measures summarise performance on the held-out set. The coefficient of determination,

$$ R^{2} \;=\; 1 - \frac{\sum_i (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}{\sum_i (y_i - \bar{y})^2}, $$

reports the fraction of variance the model explains, where unity is perfect. The mean absolute error,

$$ \text{MAE} \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \left| y_i - \hat{y}_i \right|, $$

reports the typical miss in index points. On the held-out cities the model attains $R^{2}\approx 0.99$ and an MAE of roughly $6.5$ points. We read the high $R^{2}$ with care: because the target is, by Numbeo's construction, a basket that includes the input categories, accuracy is partly structural. The scientifically interesting output is therefore the decomposition of each prediction, not its precision.

Chapter 7

Explanation: Shapley values

To attribute a prediction to its inputs we use Shapley values, a construct from cooperative game theory. Treating the features as players who cooperate to produce the prediction, the Shapley value $\phi_i$ is the unique fair allocation of the prediction to feature $i$ — its average marginal contribution across every ordering in which features might be introduced:

$$ \phi_i \;=\; \sum_{S \subseteq N \setminus \{i\}} \frac{|S|!\,\bigl(|N|-|S|-1\bigr)!}{|N|!}\,\Bigl[\, v\!\left(S \cup \{i\}\right) - v(S) \,\Bigr], $$

where $N$ is the set of features, $S$ a subset excluding $i$, and $v(S)$ the model's expected output given only the features in $S$. These values obey an additivity guarantee: they reconstruct each prediction exactly as a base value plus the sum of contributions,

$$ f(x) \;=\; \phi_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{M} \phi_i, $$

with $\phi_0$ the average prediction over the dataset — the "typical city". A positive $\phi_i$ pushes a city above that baseline; a negative one pulls it below. These are precisely the driver bars in the interface.

The exact computation is, in general, exponential in the number of features. For tree ensembles, however, the TreeSHAP algorithm yields the exact values in polynomial time — a second, decisive reason for choosing trees. Averaging the magnitude of these contributions across all cities gives a global ranking of drivers, in which rent and housing dominates, ahead of groceries and dining.

Chapter 8

Similarity search

Alongside the supervised model sits an unsupervised one. To find cities of similar cost structure, each city's cost features are standardised to zero mean and unit variance,

$$ z_{k} \;=\; \frac{x_{k} - \mu_{k}}{\sigma_{k}}, $$

and the nearest neighbours are retrieved by Euclidean distance in this standardised space,

$$ d(a,b) \;=\; \sqrt{\sum_{k} \left(z^{a}_{k} - z^{b}_{k}\right)^{2}}. $$

Standardisation is essential: without it, the distance would be dominated by whichever index happens to take the largest numerical values. With it, two cities count as similar when the shape of their costs agrees — which categories run high and which run low — rather than merely their headline total.

Chapter 9

Language-model overviews

Each city is accompanied by a short prose summary, grounded strictly in its own figures. Where an Anthropic API key is configured, the summary is composed by a large language model (Claude), prompted with the city's indices and instructed not to assert anything beyond them. Absent a key, a deterministic template assembles the same facts into fluent text, so the system remains fully functional offline. In both cases the summaries are generated once and cached, then served without latency.

Chapter 10

Geography and the map

To situate the data geographically, every city was assigned coordinates by joining the dataset against two open references — SimpleMaps for clean country codes and GeoNames for breadth of coverage and alternate spellings — achieving a complete match. The map plots only these cities, each rendered as a point coloured by total cost along a teal-to-rust scale. Selection is unified across the interface: choosing a city on the map, in the dropdown, or from the similar-cities list updates all three.

Chapter 11

Threats to validity

Intellectual honesty requires stating what the system cannot claim.

  • Observational data. Numbeo is crowd-sourced; coverage and reliability vary by city, and prices drift. The snapshot is a single point in time.
  • Association, not causation. Shapley values explain the model, which captures statistical association. They are not the effects of a controlled intervention.
  • Structural accuracy. Because the target contains its predictors, a high $R^{2}$ should be read as internal consistency, not as evidence of forecasting power from independent signals.
  • Aggregation. A single city index conceals neighbourhood-level inequality and informal economies; purchasing power mitigates but does not remove this.
