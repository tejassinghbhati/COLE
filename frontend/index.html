Understanding this view

This page estimates how expensive a city is and — more importantly — explains why. The model never just reports a number; it shows which everyday costs are responsible for it.

1

The cost-of-living index. A single score on a scale where New York City = 100. An index of 130 means living there costs about 30% more than NYC; an index of 70 means about 30% less.

2

Income & affordability burden. A high price tag is only painful if wages can't cover it. The burden divides cost by local income — around 100 is a typical balance, while higher numbers mean pay is not keeping up with prices.

3

The driver bars. Each bar is one spending category. A red bar pushes this city above the average city; a blue bar pulls it below. The longer the bar, the bigger that category's effect on the final score.

4

Similar cities. Found by matching the shape of a city's costs — which categories run high or low — not just the total. Two equally pricey cities can still be expensive for very different reasons.

Understanding this view

Two cities can land on a similar overall index for completely different reasons — one drowning in rent, the other in healthcare bills. This page pulls those reasons apart, category by category.

1

The two scores. Each card shows a city's predicted cost-of-living index and its local monthly income, side by side.

2

The diverging bars. Every bar is one cost category. A bar to the right means City A is more expensive there; to the left means City B is. The longest bars are where the real gap lives.

3

Why it's useful. Instead of a vague "A is pricier than B", you see the exact mix — maybe A's housing is far higher but B's transport and childcare claw some of it back.

Understanding this view

The other tabs look at single cities; this one zooms out to the patterns hidden across all of them — and shows how trustworthy the model is.

1

Global cost drivers. Averaged over every city, which categories move the index the most. It's the typical size of each factor's effect, so the longest bar is the lever that matters most worldwide — usually housing.

2

Regions table. Average cost, income and affordability burden by world region. It reveals that "most expensive" and "hardest to afford" are not the same list — high wages can offset high prices.

3

Model card. How well the model does on cities it never saw while learning. R² is the share of variation it explains (1.0 = perfect); MAE is the average miss, in index points.

Understanding this view

A sandbox for building a hypothetical place. Set the costs yourself and watch the model react — the best way to feel how much each factor really matters.

1

The sliders. Dial each cost category, the local income and the density to any values you like, then choose a region.

2

Predict & explain. The model estimates the overall index for your invented city and breaks down which of your settings pushed the score up or down — the same SHAP explanation used for real cities.

3

What it teaches. Move one slider at a time. You'll see that raising housing shifts the index far more than raising utilities — a direct, hands-on view of which costs dominate.
