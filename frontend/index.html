Understanding this view

This page estimates how expensive a city is relative to New Delhi and explains why. Every number is an index where New Delhi = 100, so a value of 300 means roughly three times Delhi's level.

1

The overview. A short, plain-language summary written for this specific city from its real numbers — overall cost, its biggest pressure, and how local wages compare to Delhi.

2

The metrics. Total cost of living, rent & housing, and local purchasing power — each on the New Delhi = 100 scale. Purchasing power above 100 means salaries stretch further than in Delhi.

3

The driver bars. The model attributes the cost with SHAP. A rust bar pushes this city above the typical city; a teal bar pulls it below. Longer bar = bigger effect.

4

Similar cities. Found by matching the shape of a city's costs — which categories run high or low — not merely the headline total.

Where the cost gap comes from

Each bar: how much higher (right, rust) or lower (left, teal) City A is than City B, per category.

Understanding this view

Two cities can land on a similar overall index for completely different reasons — one driven by rent, another by groceries. This page pulls those reasons apart.

1

The two scores. Each city's predicted total cost (New Delhi = 100) and its local purchasing power, side by side.

2

The diverging bars. Every bar is one cost category. Right (rust) means City A is pricier there; left (teal) means City B is. The longest bars are where the real gap lives.

3

Why it's useful. You see the exact mix behind a comparison instead of a vague "A is dearer than B".
